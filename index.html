Outdoor Ambient Air Pollution: exposure to air pollutants at low exposures for long periods of time

Here are 6 air pollutants that the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) actively measures:

Carbon Monoxide (CO)

released from mainly car, truck, and other vehicle emissions

Sulfer Dioxide (SO2)

released from mainly burning fossil fuels in industry facilities

Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2)

released from vehicle emissions and burning fossil fuels

Lead (Pb)

released into the air from metal processing plants, waste incinerators, and aircrafts using leaded aviation fuel

Ground-Level Ozone (O3)

created through reactions of nitrogen oxides, VOCs, and sunlight
--

Particulate Matter (PM)

released from sources like fires & construction sites or formed through chemical reactions of pollutants

Where do these pollutants come from?

We categorized air pollutant sources into 4 main categories:

  • Stationary Fuel Combustion: electric utilities, industrial boilers
  • Industrial and Other processes: metal smelters, petroleum refineries, cement kilns, dry cleaners
  • Highway Vehicles: cars, trucks
  • Non-road Mobile: recreational and construction equipment, marine vessels, aircraft and locomotives

How can we protect ourselves from outdoor ambient air pollution?

A review by Laumbach et al. published in 2021 looks at different personal interventions to reduce exposure such as:

N95, KN95, FFP2 Masks: 95% filtration of airborne particles of ~0.3 microns

Air Filters: ~70% filtration of PM
**Air filter effectiveness depends on placement and frequencys of use.

Outdoor air pollution affects people even when they are not outside. PM can enter through windows, vents, and other openings.

Staying indoors with windows shut: ~20% filtration of Ozone

**For PM 2.5, this strategy is not effective.

The variability on filtration depends on the building's windows, building materials, and filtration system.

Combatting Air Pollution as a Trend

With the ongoing COVID pandemic and increasing frequency of wildfires, we have become aware about the air we breathe and what we are exposing ourselves to. As a result, we have seen a pattern of consumer products around personal protection from air pollution hitting the shelves.

Dyson created their Dyson Zone, air purifying headphones, in 2022.
