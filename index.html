Outdoor Ambient Air Pollution and Health Consquences

Scroll

Living downtown Los Angeles for the past 3 years taught me a lot about air pollution. Every time I drove into LA I saw the smog that hangs over downtown. And every week a layer of dust, dirt, and ash would settle on my apartment balcony.

I didn’t really think much of it, until there was an explosion in a crude oil refinery about 13 miles away from our school. This explosion was huge, the flames were 100 feet tall, and it sent incalculable amounts of smoke, dust, and toxic pollutants into the air. The next morning I walked to class terrified. Besides the haze in the sky, I had no idea what else was spewing from that refinery and what I and the rest of LA would be inhaling that day and the weeks to come.

Since, I have become more conscious of the quality of air around me, and I think you should too.

Los Angeles, CA

Outdoor air pollution consists of many different types of particles and chemicals from a wide range of sources. Some pollutants are natural like sand or wildfire smoke and others are man-made like nitrogen oxides from vehicle emissions. Pollutants can be released from specific events, but most pollutants are released as a by-product of our society’s functions: sewage treatment plants, power plants, all factories, agriculture, and transportation.

This project focuses on outdoor ambient air pollution – the exposure to air pollutants at low exposures for long periods of time.

To help measure and regulate air pollution in the United States, President Nixon established the Environmental Protections Agency (EPA) in 1970. Their research, advocacy, and enforcement of environmental and climate legislation has led progress in keeping communities in the U.S. safe.

On air pollution, the EPA set standards on and actively monitors 6 air pollutants:

Carbon Monoxide (CO)

Released from mainly car, truck, and other vehicle emissions

Sulfer Dioxide (SO2)

Released from mainly burning fossil fuels in industry facilities

Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2)

Released from vehicle emissions and burning fossil fuels

Lead (Pb)

Released into the air from metal processing plants, waste incinerators, and aircrafts using leaded aviation fuel

Ground-Level Ozone (O3)

Created through reactions of nitrogen oxides, VOCs, and sunlight

Particulate Matter (PM)

Where do these pollutants come from?

According to the EPA’s air pollution report in 2019, they found that emissions come from four main categories.

Stationary Fuel Combustion: electric utilities, industrial boilers

Industrial and Other processes: metal smelters, petroleum refineries, cement kilns, dry cleaners

Highway Vehicles: cars, trucks

Non-road Mobile: recreational and construction equipment, marine vessels, aircraft and locomotives

Focusing on Particulate Matter (PM)

Particulate matter has been historial proxy for air pollution because it consists of a combination of organic and inorganic components.

These fine particles are so small, we inhale them without even knowing it. PM is measured by the unit, micron. This animation shows the relative size of a PM 2.5 particle compared to the diameter of a human hair.

PM 2.5 Concentrations Around the World

99% of the world is living in place where air quality is deemed not safe according to the World Health Organization (WHO, 2019).

WHO sets guidelines values on PM2.5 concentrations which should be the country's ultimate objectives. In 2005, WHO set their guideline value at 10 μg/m3, however since then the value has decreased to 5 μg/m3 in 2021.

-- PM2.5 is measured as Annual Population Weighted Average PM2.5 Concentration. This metric links the concentration measured in an area with the number of people living there.
-- Attributable Mortality measures the proportion of non-accidental deaths that were associated with air pollution. The higher the percent attributable mortality, the more air pollution plays a role in the cause of non-accidental deaths in a population.

Ambient air pollution has been found to contribute to many non-communicable diseases.

PM 2.5 has been a primary pollutant studied by public health and environmental researchers. Because of the size of PM 2.5, it enters our lungs, respiratory system, and even passes through the blood brain barrier. PM matters because it has serious health consequences. Exposures to particulate matter result in higher risk of cardiovascular complications, respiratory non-communicable diseases, and cognitive health.

people died from air pollutant related conditions.

Air pollution contributes to disease mortality.

In 2021, the Health Effect Institute (HEI) released a research report on ambient air pollution around the world and air pollution’s disease burden for certain non-communicable diseases as attributable mortality rates. This report is the first to make global estimates on population PM 2.5 exposure and global relationships between air pollution and health.
We summarize disease-specific attributable mortality findings from the McDuffie et al report from the Global Burdens of Disease 2019 CRF (concentration-response functions) model.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a lung condition where the lungs struggle to push air in and out, overall decreasing lung capacity. Air pollution has been linked to increase risks of or exacerbate COPD symptoms.

Ischemic heart disease is the buildup of plaque in heart arteries which limit blood and oxygen circulation in the body. Studies have found long-term exposure to air pollution accelerates calcium buildup in arteries.

Lower respiratory tract infections (LRI) are infections that take place in the trachea, bronchi, and lungs such as pneumonia, bronchitis, or the flu. Rates of LRI found in both children and adults have been highly correlated with air pollution.

Strokes have been found to be positively associated with increased ambient air pollution or dust storms. As air pollution worsened, an increased number of cardiovascular events occured.

How can we protect ourselves from outdoor ambient air pollution?

Researchers have looked into several strategies to reduce exposure to outdoor ambient air pollution. We look at three interventions with varying effectiveness: masks, air filters, and staying inside.

Combatting Air Pollution as a Trend

With the ongoing COVID pandemic and increasing frequency of wildfires, we have become aware about the air we breathe and what we are exposing ourselves to. As a result, we have seen a pattern of consumer products around personal protection from air pollution hitting the shelves.

Dyson created their Dyson Zone, air purifying headphones, in 2022.

Will.i.am x Honeywell partnered to created the XUPERMASK in 2021.

Molekule designed an indoor air filter with a handle, so you can carry it around?

Small portable air filters like this necklace have also been sold, but its effectiveness is unclear.
In 2019, President Rodrigo Duterte of the Philippines was seen wearing an air purifier necklace to public appearances.

LG released PuriCare™ Wearable Air Purifier in 2020.
